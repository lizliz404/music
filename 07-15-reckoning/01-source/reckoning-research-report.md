# Research Report: What Happens When All Relationships Are Purely Transactional?

**Research date**: 2026-07-15
**Trigger question**: "如果所有关系都只按价值计算，或者说人百分百遵循价值社交的逻辑，那他会变成什么样？性价比、投产比如何？风险和代价是什么？"

---

## Executive Summary

The question of what happens when someone treats all relationships as pure value calculation has been extensively studied across **sociology, game theory, social psychology, evolutionary biology, and behavioral economics**. The answer converges from two independent directions:

**From game theory and economics**: pure transactional logic is **mathematically suboptimal** in repeated social interactions. Strategies that appear "irrational" in the short term (cooperation without immediate payoff, forgiveness, non-calculated commitment) systematically outperform pure value-maximization in the long run.

**From social psychology (Clark & Mills)**: the act of calculation itself **immediately damages relationships** governed by communal norms. It's not just that calculation yields worse long-term outcomes — it's that calculation is a **norm violation** that triggers negative reactions in real time. The other person doesn't think "this person is inefficient." They think "this person doesn't see me as close."

## 1. Social Exchange Theory — The Academic Foundation

**Key figures**: George Homans, Peter Blau, John Thibaut, Harold Kelley, Richard Emerson (1950s-1970s)
**Wikipedia**: [Social exchange theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_exchange_theory)

Social Exchange Theory (SET) is the sociological and psychological framework that models human relationships as cost-benefit calculations. It posits that people evaluate relationships by weighing rewards against costs, and that relationships persist only when both parties perceive a fair return.

**Homans' core insight**: Social exchange is "the exchange of activity, tangible or intangible, and more or less rewarding or costing between at least two persons." People expect returns when they act well toward someone, and punishment when they harm another.

**The paradox in SET itself**: Even within this framework, the theory acknowledges that pure calculation breaks down. Blau distinguished between **social exchange** (diffuse, unspecified obligations, trust-based) and **economic exchange** (specified, contractual, calculable). He argued that attempting to convert social exchange into economic exchange destroys the very benefits social exchange provides — because the **obligation is intentionally left diffuse** to allow for flexibility and long-term reciprocity.

**Relevance to the question**: SET demonstrates that even the researchers who built the cost-benefit model of relationships recognized its limits. Pure economic calculation in relationships is not an "extreme version" of their theory — it's a category error.

## 2. The Prisoner's Dilemma & Iterated Games — Why Defection Is Rational Only in Isolation

**Key figures**: Merrill Flood, Melvin Dresher (1950), Albert Tucker, Robert Axelrod, W.D. Hamilton
**Wikipedia**: [Prisoner's dilemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prisoner%27s_dilemma)

The Prisoner's Dilemma models the fundamental tension in any relationship: cooperation benefits both parties, but defection (exploitation) benefits the individual — **if the interaction only happens once**.

**Single-shot game**: Defection is the dominant strategy. Each player, reasoning rationally, defects. Both end up worse off than if they had cooperated. This is the mathematical trap of pure value-maximization.

**Iterated game**: When the game repeats (and players don't know which round is the last), the calculus changes. John Nash himself observed: "rational behavior in the iterated version of the game can differ from that in a single-round version." Cooperation becomes rational because future rounds create value that outweighs the one-time gain of defection.

**The critical variable — ω (omega)**: The "shadow of the future" — the probability that the players will meet again. When ω is low (negligible chance of future interaction), defection is rational. When ω is high (ongoing relationship), cooperation is rational. **A person who treats every interaction as if ω = 0 is making a mathematical error about the structure of the game they are actually in.**

## 3. Axelrod's Tournaments — Empirical Proof That "Nice" Strategies Win

**Key work**: Robert Axelrod, *The Evolution of Cooperation* (1984); Axelrod & Hamilton (1981)
**Wikipedia**: [The Evolution of Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Evolution_of_Cooperation), [Tit for tat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tit_for_tat)

Axelrod ran computer tournaments where strategies competed in iterated Prisoner's Dilemma games. The results were definitive and surprising:

### The Winner: Tit-for-Tat (TFT)

Submitted by Anatol Rapoport, TFT is remarkably simple:
1. **Cooperate on the first move** (be nice — never defect first)
2. **Then do whatever the other player did last round** (reciprocate)

TFT won **both** tournaments against far more sophisticated strategies.

### Why TFT Won

Axelrod identified four characteristics of successful strategies:

| Trait | Description | TFT Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Be nice** | Never be the first to defect | Cooperates on move 1 |
| **Be provocable** | Retaliate immediately against defection | Defects if opponent defected last round |
| **Don't be envious** | Maximize your own score, don't obsess over relative standing | TFT can never score HIGHER than its partner |
| **Don't be too clever** | Be transparent and predictable | Simple strategy, easy for others to read |

**The critical insight**: "TFT won, not by doing better than the other player, but by **eliciting cooperation** [and] by promoting the mutual interest rather than by exploiting the other's weakness."

### The Ecological Simulation

Axelrod also ran an "ecological" tournament where strategies reproduced based on success. Results:
- **Nice strategies** came to dominate the field
- **Exploitative strategies** were initially successful against naive cooperators, but as their victims were eliminated, exploiters ran out of targets and collapsed
- **Non-provocable strategies** (always cooperate) were exploited and eliminated
- The **stable equilibrium** was a population of nice-but-provocable strategies

**Relevance to the question**: A person who treats all relationships as transactions is running an "always-defect" or "exploitative" strategy. In a single-round interaction, this might win. In the repeated game of social life, it is **provably outcompeted by strategies that cooperate first and reciprocate.**

## 4. Folk Theorem — Repeated Games Sustain Cooperation

**Wikipedia**: [Folk theorem (game theory)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folk_theorem_(game_theory))

The Folk Theorem formalizes a deep result: in infinitely repeated games (or games where the end is uncertain), **virtually any payoff profile can be sustained as a Nash equilibrium**, including mutually cooperative outcomes — provided players are sufficiently patient and farsighted.

**Implication**: Cooperation is not a departure from rationality in repeated games. It is one of many rational equilibria. The question is not "can cooperation be rational?" but "which equilibrium do the players coordinate on?" **Pure value-maximization is just one equilibrium — and not the highest-payoff one.**

## 5. Costly Signaling Theory — Why "Irrational" Gestures Build Trust

**Key figures**: Amotz Zahavi (1975), Ronald Fisher, Michael Spence
**Wikipedia**: [Signalling theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signalling_theory), [Handicap principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handicap_principle)

### The Handicap Principle (Zahavi, 1975)

In evolutionary biology, animals signal their quality through **costly displays** — the peacock's tail, the gazelle's stotting (jumping high when spotting a predator). These signals are honest precisely **because they are expensive to produce**. A weak individual cannot afford the cost, so the signal reliably indicates quality.

Mathematically: signals must be **wasteful enough** that low-quality individuals cannot afford to fake them. This is called a **separating equilibrium**.

### Application to Human Relationships

Human trust operates on the same mechanism. A **costly signal** in a relationship is an action that:
- Has no immediate payoff
- Cannot be justified by short-term ROI
- Demonstrates commitment precisely because it's "irrational"

Examples:
- Showing up when there's nothing to gain
- Making a promise that restricts future options (commitment device)
- Investing in a relationship before knowing if it will "pay off"
- Staying when a better option is available

**The paradox**: A pure value-calculator **cannot produce a credible costly signal** because every action must be justified by immediate return. They are trapped in a pooling equilibrium with actual low-commitment individuals — because their behavior is indistinguishable from someone who doesn't care.

**Relevance to the question**: The "efficiency optimization machine" cannot build trust because trust requires actions that look inefficient. The machine's transparency ("I optimize everything") makes its loyalty non-credible — because the other party can observe that the machine will switch to a better option the moment one appears.

## 6. Adverse Selection (The Market for Lemons) — How Value-Optimization Drives Away Quality

**Key work**: George Akerlof, "The Market for Lemons" (1970) — Nobel Prize 2001
**Wikipedia**: [The Market for Lemons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Market_for_Lemons)

Akerlof's model of asymmetric information in markets applies directly to transactional relationships:

### The Lemon Market Mechanism

1. Buyers cannot distinguish high-quality goods ("peaches") from low-quality goods ("lemons")
2. Buyers are only willing to pay an **average price** reflecting the market's mixed quality
3. Sellers of peaches cannot get a price that reflects true quality → they **exit the market**
4. As peaches exit, average quality drops → average price drops → more peaches exit
5. **Market death spiral**: only lemons remain

### Application to Relationships

In a social environment where everyone is calculating value:

1. High-quality relationship partners (those capable of deep, non-transactional connection) observe that they are being **priced as commodities**
2. They exit the relationship pool — they invest elsewhere, with people who don't calculate
3. The remaining pool consists of: (a) other pure calculators, and (b) exploiters who specialize in gaming transactional systems
4. The calculator's relationship environment undergoes **adverse selection**: quality drains out, manipulation enters

### Two Populations Diverge

| Population A (Calculators) | Population B (Non-calculators) |
|---|---|
| Mutual suspicion, high friction | Trust-based, low transaction costs |
| Each interaction requires explicit negotiation | Implicit reciprocity norms |
| Attracts exploiters (no loyalty floor) | Exploiters filtered out (entry cost is non-transactional commitment) |
| No tail insurance | Identity-based commitment provides tail insurance |

## 7. Bounded Rationality — Why Full Calculation Isn't Even Efficient

**Key figure**: Herbert Simon (1950s) — Nobel Prize 1978
**Wikipedia**: [Bounded rationality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_rationality)

Simon demonstrated that human decision-making operates under **cognitive constraints**: limited information, limited processing capacity, limited time. Under these constraints, the rational strategy is not optimization but **satisficing** — finding a solution that is "good enough" rather than optimal.

### The Computational Cost Problem

A person who calculates the ROI of every relationship incurs:
- **Monitoring costs**: continuously evaluating each person's current value
- **Re-pricing costs**: recalculating as circumstances change
- **Opportunity costs**: time spent calculating could be spent experiencing the relationship
- **Error costs**: bad valuations lead to bad decisions with compounding effects

**Simon's insight applied here**: "Classification heuristics" (family, old friends — don't calculate) are not emotional indulgences. They are **computationally efficient strategies** that save cognitive resources for genuinely novel decisions. A person who refuses to use heuristics and insists on per-transaction calculation is not being more rational — they are being **less efficient** by ignoring their own computational constraints.

### Overfitting the Current Context

As noted in the original analysis, pure value-optimization of relationships is **overfitting**:
- High performance on the training set (current social position, resources, status)
- Zero robustness to distribution shift (job loss, illness, life transitions)
- No regularization — no relationships maintained "because of history rather than current value"

In machine learning terms, this is a model with **no validation set** and **no regularization term** — it will catastrophically fail when conditions change.

## 8. Commitment Devices & Trust — Credibility Requires Self-Restriction

**Key figures**: Thomas Schelling, Robert Strotz (1950s)
**Wikipedia**: [Commitment device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commitment_device), [Trust (social science)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trust_(social_science))

### Schelling's Insight

A commitment is credible only when it **restricts future options**. A general who burns the bridge behind his army makes retreat impossible — this is a credible signal of commitment to fight. A general who merely "promises to fight" while keeping the bridge intact is not credible.

### Trust as Vulnerability

Trust research defines trust as: **"the willingness to become vulnerable to another party, on the presumption that the trustee will act in ways that benefit the trustor."**

Key elements:
- The trustor does **not have control** over the trustee's actions
- The outcome is **uncertain**
- Trust requires **suspension of calculation** — because if you could calculate the outcome with certainty, it wouldn't be trust

**The calculator's trap**: A person who insists on controlling and calculating every interaction **cannot experience trust** — because trust is definitionally the suspension of calculation and control. They cannot receive trust either, because they haven't demonstrated vulnerability (a costly signal).

## 9. Reciprocal Altruism — The Evolutionary Basis

**Key figure**: Robert Trivers (1971)
**Wikipedia**: [Reciprocal altruism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_altruism)

Trivers formalized how cooperation evolves between non-kin: an organism temporarily reduces its fitness to help another, with the expectation of future reciprocation. This is the evolutionary basis of friendship.

### The Bootstrap Problem of Trust

As noted in the original analysis, any trust relationship has a **bootstrapping problem**: the first move must be a **costly, unmonitored investment** — a "leap of faith" that cannot be justified by the track record that doesn't exist yet.

A pure value-calculator:
- Requires ROI data before investing
- ROI data can only be generated by investing first
- **Deadlock**: never takes the first step, never enters any trust relationship

They are stuck at round 0 of every potential relationship. The strategies that reach high-value equilibria all start with **uncollateralized cooperation on round 1.**

## 10. Nowak's Five Mechanisms of Cooperation

**Key work**: Martin Nowak (2006), *Science*
**Wikipedia**: See [The Evolution of Cooperation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Evolution_of_Cooperation)

Nowak identified five mechanisms by which natural selection produces cooperation:

| Mechanism | Description | Relevance to Value-Only Social Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Kin selection** | Cooperation with genetic relatives | Not under strategic choice |
| **Direct reciprocity** | I help you, you help me (requires repeated interaction) | **Destroyed by pure calculation** — never initiates |
| **Indirect reciprocity** | Reputation: I help you, others help me because they observed | **Destroyed** — observable value-calculation damages reputation |
| **Network reciprocity** | Cooperation clusters in social networks | **Excluded from clusters** — networks filter out calculators |
| **Group selection** | Cooperative groups outcompete non-cooperative groups | **Self-segregates** into low-trust, low-return groups |

A pure value-calculator is cut off from mechanisms 2-5. They are restricted to mechanism 1 (kin) and one-shot market transactions.

## 11. Synthesis: What the Research Says

### The Core Finding

Across disciplines, the research converges on a single counterintuitive conclusion: **strategies that locally appear to sacrifice value are globally value-maximizing**. The "irrational" behaviors — uncalculated generosity, commitment without exit clauses, forgiveness after betrayal, investment without guaranteed return — are not exceptions to rational behavior. They are the **rational strategy for an agent who correctly models the game as iterated, the horizon as uncertain, and the payoff structure as non-zero-sum.**

### What the Pure Value-Calculator Gets Wrong

They are solving the wrong game. They model social life as a series of **independent single-shot transactions** (where defection is rational), when social life is a **repeated game with uncertain horizon and non-zero-sum payoffs** (where cooperation is rational).

This is a **modeling error**, not a values error. The calculator isn't "too cold" — they're solving the wrong math problem.

### Predicted Outcome

| Dimension | Outcome for Pure Value-Calculator |
|---|---|
| **Short-term efficiency** | High — each transaction optimized |
| **Long-term returns** | Low — excluded from high-trust, high-return networks |
| **Adverse selection** | Systematic — quality partners exit, exploiters enter |
| **Tail risk** | Catastrophic — no identity-based safety net |
| **Computational overhead** | High — constant re-pricing drains cognitive resources |
| **Credible commitment** | Impossible — no costly signal capability |
| **Trust** | Neither given nor received |
| **Identity continuity** | No external anchors — self-narrative unverified |

### The Inescapable Irony

The pure value-calculator, in attempting to maximize value from every interaction, **destroys the conditions under which the highest-value interactions are possible**. They optimize themselves into a local maximum and cannot reach the global maximum — which requires taking the "irrational" first step of uncalculated investment.

This is not a moral argument. It is a mathematical property of iterated games with uncertain horizons and costly information. The calculator is not a bad person. They are running a strategy that is **provably dominated** in the actual game they are playing.

## 12. Communal vs. Exchange Relationships — The Social Psychology Pillar

**Key figures**: Margaret Clark & Judson Mills (1979–present)
**Key works**: Clark & Mills (1979), *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*; Clark & Mills (1993), *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*; extensive replication through 2020s

### The Fundamental Distinction

Clark & Mills identified two qualitatively different norms governing human relationships:

| | **Exchange Relationships** | **Communal Relationships** |
|---|---|---|
| **Norm** | Quid pro quo — track who gave what, expect equivalent return | Give based on the other's **need/welfare**, not tracking debt |
| **Accounting** | Strict mental ledger maintained | Deliberately **not** tracking — accounting would violate the norm |
| **Typical contexts** | Business, acquaintances, strangers, professional transactions | Close friends, romantic partners, family, kin-like bonds |
| **Giving motive** | To obtain equivalent return or repay debt | Because the other person needs it |
| **Receiving response** | Expected: reciprocal return | Expected: gratitude, not debt calculation |

### The Critical Experimental Finding

Clark & Mills ran experiments where participants were placed in communal or exchange contexts. In communal conditions:

- **Strict accounting of favors decreased liking and relationship satisfaction**
- When a partner immediately offered equivalent repayment for a favor, **attraction dropped** — the repayment itself signaled "I see this as a transaction, not a relationship"
- Participants in communal conditions **avoided keeping score** even when it would have been to their advantage

**The mechanism is norm violation, not efficiency loss.** It's not that calculation is "suboptimal" in the long run — it's that calculation **immediately violates the relationship type's governing norm**, triggering negative reactions in real time. The other person doesn't think "this person is inefficient." They think "this person doesn't see me as close."

### Communal and Exchange Orientation Scale (COS)

Clark and colleagues developed the **Communal and Exchange Orientation Scale** to measure individual differences:

- **High exchange orientation**: chronically monitors fairness and repayment in relationships. Tracks who gave what, expects proportional returns.
- **High communal orientation**: gives based on perceived need, uncomfortable with strict accounting in close relationships.

**Key empirical findings with COS**:

- High exchange orientation is **significantly negatively correlated** with romantic relationship satisfaction
- **Longitudinal studies**: increases in exchange orientation predict **subsequent declines** in relationship satisfaction over time
- **Mediation**: the effect is partially mediated by **reduced gratitude** — exchange-oriented individuals experience and express less gratitude, because they frame support as "debt to be repaid" rather than "gift freely given"
- During conflict, high-exchange partners show **lower intimacy**, more defensive accounting ("I did X, so you owe me Y")
- High-exchange individuals have **fewer close friendships** and report lower overall well-being

### Why This Matters for the "Efficiency Machine"

Clark & Mills provide the **direct experimental counter-evidence** to the pure value-calculation strategy. The Axelrod tournaments show that cooperation beats exploitation in repeated games — but Clark & Mills show something more fundamental: **the act of calculation itself damages communal relationships**, regardless of the calculated outcome.

A person who treats all relationships as exchange relationships is not just choosing a suboptimal strategy within a game. They are **misclassifying the game type entirely** — applying exchange norms to relationships that operate under communal norms. The damage is not deferred to the long run; it happens at the moment of misclassification.

## 13. Network Theory — What the Efficiency Machine Prioritizes and What It Loses

**Key figures**: Mark Granovetter (1973), Robert Putnam (2000)

### Granovetter's Weak Ties

Granovetter's classic *"The Strength of Weak Ties"* (1973) demonstrated that **weak ties** (acquaintances, loose connections) are disproportionately valuable for **novel information** — job opportunities, new ideas, resource access. Strong ties (close friends, family) tend to share redundant information because they occupy overlapping social circles.

### What the Calculator Does

A pure value-calculator, optimizing for information and resource access, will naturally:
- **Prioritize weak ties** — they have higher instrumental ROI per interaction
- **Prune strong ties** — they appear "low-ROI" because their value is non-instrumental and non-incremental
- **Build bridging capital** at the expense of bonding capital

### Putnam's Bonding vs. Bridging Capital

| | **Bridging Capital** | **Bonding Capital** |
|---|---|---|
| **Function** | Connects across groups, brings novel info/resources | Provides emotional support, safety net, identity |
| **Network type** | Weak ties, diverse contacts | Strong ties, close-knit groups |
| **Calculator's behavior** | Maximizes this | Neglects or eliminates this |
| **Cost of loss** | — | No safety net in crisis, no identity continuity, no unconditional support |

**The calculator's trap**: They build an impressive network of bridging capital — a wide circle of useful contacts — but have **no bonding capital**. When crisis hits (job loss, illness, breakdown), bridging capital evaporates because it was maintained by instrumental value, not by commitment. The calculator discovers their network is **wide but zero-depth** — a mile wide, an inch deep.

### Reputation Effects

Once identified as a pure calculator, the person's reputation propagates through networks. Future potential partners observe the pattern and preemptively limit investment. The calculator faces a **reputation tax**: every new relationship starts from a deficit of trust, because their type has been signaled.

## 14. Physiological Costs — What the Body Pays

The absence of high-quality communal relationships has measurable physiological consequences, documented in health psychology and social neuroscience:

| **Outcome** | **Evidence** |
|---|---|
| **Loneliness** | Chronically elevated in individuals with predominantly exchange-based relationships |
| **Depression & anxiety** | Higher risk; communal support is a known protective factor |
| **Cardiovascular stress** | Elevated blood pressure, higher resting heart rate, increased inflammation markers (CRP, IL-6) |
| **Cortisol dysregulation** | Flatter diurnal cortisol slopes — a marker of chronic stress — in socially isolated individuals |
| **All-cause mortality** | Social integration is as strong a predictor of mortality as smoking, obesity, and physical inactivity (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2010 meta-analysis) |
| **Immune function** | Weaker immune response; slower wound healing in those with fewer close relationships |

**The irony**: The calculator optimizes for "value" but incurs **physiological costs that reduce their capacity to produce value** — worse health, lower energy, shorter lifespan. The body keeps a ledger the mind refuses to acknowledge.

## 15. The Norm-Violation Mechanism — Why Calculation Hurts Immediately

A crucial insight from Clark & Mills that differs from the game-theoretic argument:

**Game theory** says: "cooperation is rational in repeated games, so calculation is a long-run mistake."

**Social psychology** says: "calculation is a **norm violation** that produces **immediate negative reactions** in communal contexts. The damage is not deferred — it happens the moment the other person detects they are being accounted for."

This has practical implications for the "efficiency machine":
- They cannot "strategically cooperate" to get long-term returns, because **even strategic cooperation is detectable** when it's contingent and tracked
- True communal behavior requires **non-contingency** — giving without tracking, supporting without calculating
- The calculator cannot fake this, because the norm violation is in the **accounting itself**, not in the decision outcome

### Connection to Costly Signaling

Clark & Mills' framework provides the missing mechanism for why costly signals work: a gift in a communal relationship is a signal that **you are not in exchange mode**. The "cost" is not the gift's material value — it's the **deliberate refusal to account for it**. By not tracking what you're owed, you signal: "this is communal, not exchange."

The calculator cannot produce this signal because **not-tracking is precisely what they are incapable of doing.**
