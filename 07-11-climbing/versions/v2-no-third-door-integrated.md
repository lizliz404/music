Upbeat electronic hip-hop with a melodic pop-rap chorus, 110 BPM, bright G major.
Male vocalist: confident rapper-singer with a playful, self-deprecating grin —
urban edge, slight rasp, never aggressive, never brooding. Journey: intimate
spoken-word intro over sparse kick, warm filtered synth pulse, distant footstep
foley. Verse 1 snaps in — crisp 808s, bouncy synth stabs like climbing footsteps,
locked-in rap groove. Pre-chorus lifts with stacked harmonies and building pads.
Chorus explodes — wide supersaw chords, punchy four-on-the-floor drums, belted
anthemic hook with gang-vocal chant on the tag line. Verse 2 tighter rap pocket,
denser internal rhymes, more syncopation. Bridge strips to half-time — airy
pads, breathy near-falsetto, sparse percussion, contemplative. Final chorus
detonates — full production, choir-layered harmonies, escalated variation on the
hook (not a repeat). Outro fades to spoken word over synth drone and footsteps,
warm smiling close. Production: clean modern electronic, analog warmth on the
bass, rhythmic step-motif woven through the low end — sovereign, kinetic,
city-at-sunset energy, zero melancholy.

Exclude styles: dark, sad, lo-fi chill, trap mumble, metal, acoustic folk,
minor-key ballad, dramatic orchestral.

2. "Legs are the root" — make it more medically precise?
   ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
   This line sits at the intersection of your actual preventive-medicine
   background and the song's thesis. "Root" as metaphor is clean but generic.
   The clinical reality is more interesting: leg strength (quadriceps power,
   gait speed) is one of the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality and
   fall risk in aging populations — real correlation, not poetic license.
   
   Options if you want to lean in:
   - "Legs hold the baseline — if they fail, we fail" (baseline = clinical term)
   - "Legs are the chassis — if they crack, we crash" (mechanical, visceral)
   - Keep "root" — clean, singable, the medical weight is implied for those
     who know. Sometimes understatement IS the flex.

3. capex vs opex — push the accounting metaphor further?
   ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   Current: "This body is cash flow — not a vault you fill once"
   
   More precise alternative:
   "This body is opex — not a capex you spend once"
   (opex = operating expenditure, ongoing; capex = capital expenditure, one-time)
   
   Pros: More accurate to the concept. Separates this song from every generic
         "health is wealth" metaphor. Insider knowledge flex.
   Cons: "opex/capex" are jargon. Harder to sing. Most listeners won't parse
         them on first pass — and in a chant chorus, clarity is everything.
   
   "Cash flow / vault" is already more precise than 99% of pop lyrics.
   The question is whether the extra 1% of precision is worth the
   singability cost. Your ear, your call.
