PHASE 1: STRUCTURED AUDIT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. WHAT / WHY / HOW

What the song is doing:
"Edit Rights" narrates the experience of spending 90 yuan for one night
in a luxury residential compound and discovering that wealth isn't about
possessions — it's about the power to delete friction, noise, and chaos
from one's visible world. The song traces an emotional arc from
scarcity-induced exhaustion through wonder and analysis to ignition.

Why v1 works (across both versions):
The core concept is genuinely fresh. "Edit rights" as a metaphor for
class privilege is more penetrating than "money" or "luxury" — it names
the mechanism (power over perception) rather than the surface (stuff).
The concrete images from source materials — magnetic doors, green-plate
cars, fake vines on basement pipes, KFC coupons next to gold bars — provide
ASMR-like texture and specificity that reward relistening.

How each version achieves its effects:

  VERSION A (Lyric/Kimi) — ABABCB + Outro, 82 lines, male vocal
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Mechanism: Accumulation of documentary detail → pre-chorus as analytical
  hinge → clever pun hook ("the real estate") → cold outro ("yet").
  Strengths: pre-chorus creates valuable breathing room between verse
  density and chorus release; best single line across both versions —
  "I stood in that frame like a glitch in the code" — is a crisp,
  earned metaphor. The outro's single-word "yet" is masterful restraint.
  Weaknesses: some lines tip into essayistic ("no moral debt / While I'm
  still negotiating what I deserve to get"); "money like it's nothing,
  like it's air, like it's steam" dilutes impact through simile stacking;
  the hook ("the real estate") is clever but cerebral — it rewards
  reading more than singing.

  VERSION B (Grok 4.5 via Cursor) — ABABCB, 53 lines, female vocal
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Mechanism: Compression → aphoristic density → clinical female vocal
  persona → declarative hook → hard cut ending.
  Strengths: the single best line conceptually — "Abundance walks soft —
  scarcity keeps scorecards" — is aphoristic, penetrating, and truest to
  the source material's 配得感/匮乏感 tension. The compact structure
  improves Suno generation reliability. "I mean it today" is a strong
  declarative close. Female vocal persona ("dry-eyed observer who catalogs
  details like evidence") is more distinctive than A's documentary narrator.
  Weaknesses: no pre-chorus means less breathing room between narrative
  and release; the bridge (4 lines) is truncated — good content, not
  enough space to land; "money like cream" is the weakest simile in
  either version; some internal rhymes feel forced ("soft dream / like
  cream").

Cross-version weaknesses (both versions share these):
  (a) Neither integrates the sleep-experiment material — the scarcity-
      fighting angle. Liz's 300-entry sleep log is the other half of the
      story: someone who CANNOT edit their environment, fighting obsessively
      for basic comfort. This is the emotional counterweight to the
      compound's frictionless order, and it's entirely absent.
  (b) The "auntie" and "Toronto student" anecdotes appear in both but
      feel appended rather than structurally essential. They're evidence
      without being architecture.
  (c) Neither hook is genuinely SINGABLE. Both are declarative phrases
      you might remember intellectually, but neither creates the rhythmic-
      melodic earworm that makes a listener hum unconsciously on the train.
  (d) The "night market" as contrast appears in both but is underused —
      it's mentioned, not inhabited. It should be the narrator's HOME
      territory, not just a foil.
  (e) Both risk 曲高和寡 (too literary) — the observation-to-analysis ratio
      tilts too far toward analysis. The listener needs more physiology
      (body memory, sound texture) and less sociology.

2. CLASSIFICATION

This song belongs to: Narrative social-realism pop — a subgenre that
uses concrete class observation to build emotional resonance.

  Genre mechanics:
  - Spoken-word adjacency (documentary detail, conversational cadence)
  - Lo-fi electronic / alt R&B production palette
  - Social commentary without didacticism
  - Emotional arc driven by observation → recognition → transformation

  Comparable spiritual territory (not sound-alikes):
  - The Streets' narrative density and class specificity
  - Lorde's minimalism and outsider gaze
  - Early James Blake's use of negative space and vulnerability
  - Arlo Parks' observational intimacy

  What this is NOT:
  - Industrial pop (no formulaic ABABCB-verse-chorus predictability)
  - Trap/hip-hop (the vocal persona is too observational, not performative)
  - Pure poetry set to music (it must SING, not just read well)
  - Singer-songwriter folk (production palette is electronic, urban)

3. ITERATE VS STOP VERDICT

VERDICT: ITERATE — ONE MORE ROUND, THEN STOP.

The v1 versions are approximately 75% realized. The concept is strong
enough to deserve a synthesis that addresses the identified gaps, but the
marginal returns after one more focused round will diminish sharply.

Marginal-return inflection point:
  - A v2 can add: the scarcity-perspective opening (sleep experiment),
    a genuinely singable hook, a more structurally essential bridge, and
    fuller emotional arc (observation → overwhelm → analysis → ignition).
  - A v3 would add: fine-tuning individual lines, which is perfectionism.
    Art IS subjective. The v1-to-v2 gap is real; the v2-to-v3 gap would
    be microscopic and not worth the creative energy.

What v2 must do differently:
  1. OPEN IN THE NARRATOR'S SCARCITY WORLD. The song cannot start in the
     compound — it must earn the contrast. The sleep-experiment material
     (bad mattress, garbage trucks, noise through thin walls, the desperate
     logging of 300 nights) establishes the "before" picture.
  2. WRITE A SINGABLE HOOK. Not a clever phrase to read — a melodic earworm.
     Something with rhythmic pocket, vowel-forward syllables, and built-in
     repetition. The "edit rights" concept needs a musical delivery system.
  3. MAKE THE BRIDGE CARRY THE EMOTIONAL CORE. Bridge should be one image
     (fake vine on pipe) + one question (why not me?) — not an inventory
     of observations. The bridge is where analysis becomes ignition.
  4. END WITH TRANSFORMATION, NOT RESOLUTION. The narrator returns to the
     noise — but changed. Not "I made it" (false) or "I'm still stuck"
     (defeatist) but "I'm building what I can while I move."
  5. BALANCE LITERARINESS + AUDITORY ADDICTIVENESS. Useful (安定感, release)
     + Interesting (意外之喜, catchiness). Verses can be dense because the
     chorus provides the release valve. The ratio should be: verses reward
     close listening; chorus rewards humming.

4. MARKET RESONANCE

WILL THIS CONNECT BEYOND NICHE LISTENERS? YES — with the right execution.

Why:
  - Class anxiety and housing precarity are globally resonant themes.
    Everyone has walked past a luxury compound and felt that twinge.
  - The specific images (magnetic doors, fake vines, KFC coupons, garbage
    trucks at 5:55) are vivid enough to create ASMR-like texture even for
    listeners who don't parse the class analysis.
  - "Why not me?" is a universally accessible question — it works whether
    the listener is 18 or 45, in Shanghai or São Paulo.
  - The "useful" dimension (安定感/释怀感) comes from the song's honesty:
    it doesn't pretend the narrator has arrived. It sits in the wanting.
    That's comforting — permission to want without having.

Risk factors:
  - If too conceptual → 小众白噪音 (niche white noise for the overeducated)
  - If too formulaic → Sheeran-mean industrial pop (soulless catchiness)
  - The sweet spot: a hook simple enough to hum unconsciously + verses
    dense enough to reward the third listen. The song should work as
    background (mood, texture, groove) AND foreground (lyrics, story, arc).

Target listener profile:
  - Young urban professionals (22-35) in tier-1/2 cities
  - Late-night commuters, insomniacs, overthinkers
  - Anyone who has felt the gap between where they live and where they
    glimpsed a different life
  - Listeners who want music that acknowledges their fatigue without
    wallowing in it

SUNO STYLE PROMPT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Style:
Alt-pop with lo-fi electronic textures, 95 BPM, E minor. Female vocal —
tired eyes, sharp tongue — close-mic and conversational in verses like
3AM confessions over bad coffee. Verse 1: sparse muted guitar, dry room
ambience, a body alone with its exhaustion. Verse 2: ambient pad enters,
sub-bass pulse — the wonder of stepping into frictionless space. Chorus:
warm analog synth, gated clap on the backbeat, stacked vocal doubles on
"edit rights" — lifts into a clear declarative melody, claiming not
belting. Verse 3: clipped guitar, tight 808 — harder observational edge.
Bridge: single piano note + distant night-market hum + bare vulnerable
vocal — unresolved. Final section: distorted low-end creeps in, layered
harmonies build, then hard cut to silence — door closes, no fade. Finger
snaps on 2&4 in bridge. Production: dry verses, widening stereo reverb
in chorus. Arc: exhaustion → wonder → claim → analysis → vulnerability →
ignition → cut.

Exclude:
Trap hi-hat rolls, EDM drops, auto-tune, belting, power ballad, country
twang, orchestral swell, dubstep, fade-out ending, gospel runs, chipmunk
pitched vocals, stadium reverb, major-key brightness, sentimental strings,
lo-fi chillhop, overly sweet pop production, male vocal
